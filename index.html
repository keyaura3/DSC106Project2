Proposition: Most Complaints Are Substantiated

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Design Choice: Use a pie chart.
    • Score: 0.5 (slightly deceptive)
    • Rationale: Pie charts exaggerate proportion visually, making the substantiated cases seem bigger even if its not a majority.
  • Design Choice: Label only two categories ("Substantied" and "Other")
    • Score: -1.5 (deceptive)
    • Rationale: Aggregating all non-substatiated outcomes into one "Other" group hides the diversity of outcomes.
  • Design Choice: Affirming color for 'Substantiated' (blue)
    • Score: 0 (neutral)
    • Rationale: Using a calm color like blue makes the substantiated cases appear more trustworthy and positive.
  • Design Choice: Title: "Majority of Complaints are Substantiated."
    • Score: -1 (deceptive)
    • Rationale: Title directly asserts a majority, even if the true numbers are closer to parity.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Design Choice: Use a bar chart.
    • Score: 2 (fully earnest)
    • Rationale:Bar chart accurately represent categorical data and differences between outcomes.
  • Design Choice: Sort all board dispositions individually (no grouping).
    • Score: 2 (fully earnest)
    • Rationale: Showing every disposition seperatly highlights how "Substantiated" is just one among many outcomes.
  • Design Choice:Non-emotional color palette.
    • Score: 1 (earnest)
    • Rationale:Neutral colors reduce emotional bias, focusing attention on values.
  • Design Choice: Title: "Most Complaints Are Not Substantiated."
    • Score: -1 (deceptive)
    • Rationale: Strong wording guides the viewer to a conclusion, even though the difference might be modest.

Final Reflection

This was a really cool project to see how much visual and contextual framing of the same information can influence the perception of the data.

At first, I believed ethical visualization simply meant accurate visualization. But, after completing the checkpoint, I realized that even without changing the data, small design choices (like grouping categories or adjusting labels) can have a big impact on how the data is perceived and can lead to very different interpretations.

Finding the line between persuasiona and deception proved to be difficult. I would define ethical visualzations as one that does not intentionally mislead/hide relevant information or overstate conclusions, even if the design aims to persuade to viewer. A visualization becomes misleading when it conceals complexity, aggregates imortant information, or uses emotional language that is inconsistent with the data to sway the viewer.

From this project, I learned that ethical visualization demands transparency - not just technical accuracy.
